The urge
"I should just check in."
Do this instead
Set a timer for 10 minutes. If you still want to text when it goes off, send something warm and low pressure. Most of the time, the urge will pass on its own.
The word "should" is a signal. It means the urge is coming from habit, not from a genuine desire to connect. Habits can be redirected.
The good news: you now have 10 minutes to eat whatever you want without anyone asking "what's for dinner."

The urge
"What if something happened?"
Do this instead
Ask yourself: Is there any actual evidence something is wrong? If no, this is your brain running a worry loop. Place your hand on your chest and say: "They are okay. I am okay."
The worry loop is your nervous system scanning for threats that are not there. It is a leftover pattern from years of being responsible for their safety.
They survived your cooking. They survived learning to drive. They are handling it.

The urge
"The house is too quiet."
Do this instead
Put on your favorite music or a podcast. Walk to a different room. The quiet is not the enemy, but you can change the landscape while you adjust to it.
Silence after years of noise creates a sensory gap. Your brain interprets the gap as something wrong. It is not. It is just unfamiliar.
You can finally listen to your music at full volume. Nobody is going to come out of their room and say "Mom, seriously?"

The urge
"I just want to hear their voice."
Do this instead
Write down what you would say to them. Then read it back. If it still feels like something you want to send from a calm place, schedule a call for tomorrow.
The urge to hear their voice often spikes at night when your defenses are down. Writing it first helps you separate connection from comfort-seeking.
Bonus: they are more likely to pick up when it is not the third call today.

The urge
"They haven't texted back."
Do this instead
Put your phone in a different room for 30 minutes. Do one thing with your hands: cook, clean, garden, draw. When you come back, check once. Then move on.
Waiting for a reply activates the same brain circuitry as anticipating a reward. The longer you wait, the more urgent it feels. Breaking the physical proximity to the phone breaks the loop.
They are not ignoring you. They are probably watching a video of a cat doing something ridiculous. Their priorities are different now.

"I should have done more."
Do this instead
Write down 3 things you did well as a parent. Not big things. Small ones. The lunches you packed. The drives you made. The times you showed up when it was hard. You did enough.
Regret is a normal part of transition. Your brain reviews the past when the present feels uncertain. But regret is not truth. It is a feeling looking for evidence.
You kept them alive, fed, and mostly on time. That deserves a trophy, or at least an uninterrupted bath.

SOS Card
The urge
"Maybe I should just call."
Do this instead
Write down why you want to call. If the reason is "because I feel anxious," do 5 minutes of deep breathing first. If the reason is "because I love them and have something to share," go ahead and call. The difference matters.
The impulse to call usually means the anxiety has peaked. Pausing for 5 minutes lets the wave crest and pass. If the desire is still there after the pause, it is real connection, not a loop.
The real test: if you can wait 5 minutes, you are in charge. If you cannot, the loop is. Either way, you are learning.
